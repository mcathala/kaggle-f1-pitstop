II · The Data

Four seasons, 26 races, 439k laps.

Beyond a noisy, synthetic season, one thing really drives a pit: strategy, and the engineered features that turn it into usable signal.

II.1● THE BRIEF

The data I was handed.

One row is one lap of one driver. The target, PitNextLap, is a yes/no: does this driver pit on the next lap? I was handed 439k training laps and 188k test laps, covering 887 drivers over 26 races and four seasons, in 16 columns. About 20% are pit-laps. The data is synthetic, built from real F1 telemetry, so it behaves like an actual season. The odd generator glitch still shows, like an almost pit-less 2023. The label itself is noisy: what my models get wrong doesn't track any feature I could build (ρ≤0.012) — the residual is irreducible, not signal left on the table. You can see that ceiling on the leaderboard, where the whole top clusters within ~0.0005 AUC of the leader.

II.2● THE SIGNAL THAT CARRIES

What actually predicts a pit: strategy.

That strategy lives in tyre age, lap, stint, and what the rest of the field is doing — but no single raw column captures it: ρ=0.27 makes TyreLife the best of them, the rest below. So I engineered 49 new columns from the raw 16 to surface it — the strongest, field_pit_share (how much of the field is pitting now), alone separates a 2.2% pit rate from 38.2%. The signal lives in the features I built, not the raw inputs.

III · The Models

What I shipped, and why I took this path.

How the model is built and the blend I shipped, and the climb from baseline to final with the reasoning behind every step.

III.1● WHAT I SHIPPED

The final submission: a 7-model blend, two-thirds RealMLP.

It combines both worlds: one tuned tabular neural network anchors the blend, and machine-learning models (gradient-boosted trees) fill the residual. Why blend at all? Models that fail differently cancel each other's noise, so the average lands above any single member. It only pays when they disagree, and here diversity is scarce, so the blend stays small and deliberate.

Why three families, seven models

×3 · neural net

RealMLP

A tuned MLP that learns a per-driver embedding — driver style, the residual the trees can only target-encode. The strongest family: every tree-led blend scored below it, so it earns two-thirds of the weight.

×2 · boosted trees

XGBoost

Rule-based splits. The one fix that mattered here: raising its bin resolution (max_bin) so it stopped blurring the high-resolution category features.

×2 · boosted trees

CatBoost

Native categorical handling — suits the 887 drivers without manual encoding, and is decorrelated enough from XGBoost to earn its own slot.

Multiple copies per family add robustness, not redundancy. RealMLP is a 6-seed average (which cancels run-to-run wobble), plus a GPU pseudo-label variant and a single seed. The trees each add a lean and a decorrelated variant. All seven use the lean diffFE feature set and train on the competition data plus a leak-checked public F1-strategy set. Those +101k rows earned their place: removing them lowered the score. The weights come from a constrained coordinate-descent search, not a greedy free-for-all, so the two pseudo-label variants earn their slots on diversity, not a score bump.

How the model is built — and where the weight goes

weights set by coordinate descent on the 5-fold out-of-fold predictions, then frozen — the only honest surface while the test labels stay hidden. the split is stratified by Year×PitNextLap and audited leak-free. it's stratified, not grouped by driver, so OOF runs a touch optimistic, which is why I re-checked every gain on the real board. the climb below is 88 logged experiments over ~19 cycles, each one a hypothesis.

IV · The Right Path

The top of the board found the same three levers I did.

When the competition closed, three patterns recurred in every strong solution, and I'd built on all three. The only gap was scale: seven models against the board's 186 to 218.

IV.1● WHAT THE TOP OF THE BOARD SHARED

Three patterns recur in every top solution.

When the competition closed, I read the strongest solutions on the board. There was no secret feature, no exotic model, just three levers I'd already built on. The strongest entries stacked anywhere from 186 to 218 base models (trees, MLPs, transformer-style tabular nets — one even folded in a GNN for diversity), finished with a linear meta-learner on logits, and found their real edge in how they used the original data and absorbed its distribution shift. I reached the same leaderboard band with a deliberate 7-model blend, a constrained, regularised blend, and a lean diffFE feature set. The one axis I didn't match was scale. But on this noise ceiling, all those extra models bought only ≈+0.0008 AUC. I found the path. The rest was compute.

Pattern 03 · The real edge

Lean on the original data

diffFE subtract, don't pile on

✓mine — a lean feature set

The edge lived in how you used the original data and absorbed its distribution shift — the top of the board did it on top of heavy feature engineering. diffFE was my leaner route to the same instinct: drop the inputs that don't transfer — stripping the heavy cross-category block lifted every model (XGBoost 0.95263→0.95291).

IV.2● CHOOSING THE FINAL SUBMISSION

The highest offline score wasn't the one I shipped.

My highest offline score wasn't a single model — it was a greedy blend at OOF 0.95479, and shipping it looked like the obvious move. Greedy selection exists to squeeze exactly that number: start empty, keep adding whichever model (repeats allowed) most improves the OOF score, then average the picks — same models as my other blend, just weighted to chase the offline metric as hard as possible. That's also why I didn't trust it: near the noise ceiling the offline score oversells — a bump is as likely to be the blend fitting noise as real signal — and chasing it that hard invites exactly that. The public board, a visible slice of the test set, agreed: greedy came back lower at 0.95380, under my constrained blend's 0.95402, so I shipped the constrained one. At the close, the hidden private board confirmed the call — 0.95427 to greedy's 0.95408.

The two blends side by side, across all three columns: my constrained blend (shipped) and the higher-offline greedy one I left off. Greedy tops the offline column and loses both columns that actually count:

Shipped vs greedy — offline, public, private

Both came from the same weight optimiser over the same models — the shipped blend constrained / regularised, greedy not. Folding greedy back in wouldn't help: same bases, so it only re-loosens those weights toward the version that scored lower on the board. That one constraint is the whole difference between the offline leader and the model that held up.

What I'd try next

  • Grow the model zoo. The top of the board ran 186–218 base models; I ran 7 — more families would add the uncorrelated errors the empty quadrant was missing.
  • Finish with a logit-stack, not fixed weights. The meta-learner every strong solution used; my constrained blend is the same family, one step short.
  • Mine the original data harder. The consistent edge at the top — more shift-aware, target-encoded views of the real F1 dataset.
